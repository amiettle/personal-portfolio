UX/UI Design

Time Capsule Mobile App

Summary

Recap is a digital time capsule app that allows users to organize and store collections of photos, videos, audio recordings, and text notes. The capsules are collaborative so users can share and work on them with friends, and once a capsule is finished the user can lock it and choose a date and time for it to be reopened.

Details

Team

Amie L., Annesa T., Cristy G., Kate M.

Scope

6 weeks (February - March 2022)

Tools

Figma, Miro, Sketch, Illustrator

Research

To confirm the assumptions we had about the problem space, I performed a competitive analysis. This also helped me to identify the main features that competitors are offering and find a unique value proposition (UVP) for ReCap. The competitor lineup consisted of Google Photos, Apple's Shared Albums, and VSCO.

I compared these apps based on four aspects: Collaboration, Social Interaction, Editing Tools, and Search Tool. The features we decided to incorporate based on this analysis were:

  • Activity page: View recent interactions and keep up with friends within the app.
  • Shared Capsules: Add collaborators to a capsule and store shared memories together.
  • Photo and Video Editing: Adjust or enhance photos and videos captured within the app before storing in a capsule.
  • Search bar: Find capsule by name or collaborator.

ReCap offers the main features that competitors are providing along with greater customization options and the ability to store different file types, which sets it apart from the crowd.

Ideation

To flesh out the concept of our app further, we mapped out all of the features to be included in our design based on the insights we gathered through the competitive analysis and informal user interviews. These features were translated into functional use cases for our app.

We then set off individually and prepared rough sketches of what the app should look like to discuss the features that we liked and what could be improved or expanded upon. After we all received feedback on our designs, we were able to finalize decisions about the mapping of the functionalities and the architecture of the app.

Design

We started off with wireframes for each of the main use cases: 1) Browse time capsules, 2) Create new time capsule, 3) Add media to time capsule, 4) Give edit permissions to others, 5) Lock time capsule.

From there, we discussed how to improve the layout and visual appeal of each screen and made revisions accordingly. In particular, the layout of the home page was initially imagined to have sections for recent and shared capsules highlighted at the top. However, we later realized that it would be more intuitive and generalizable to categorize the capsules according to their state (in progress, locked, and unlocked).

The login and signup pages were designed to embody the branding and purpose of the app: to send users back in time where they can revisit past memories. I incorporated retro and space themed elements to allude to the experience of creating and opening a time capsule. To add a thoughtful touch, I also designed an onboarding carousel that greets our new users with a brief overview of the app and fun illustrations.

Evaluate

We performed usability testing with 10 participants to evaluate the user flows for onboarding, capsule creation, and locking a capsule. An error chart was used to keep track of errors made during each testing session. We found that 6 of our participants  completed all of the flows successfully, 3 experienced issues in one flow, and 1 experienced issues in two flows. We also discovered that two of our participants felt that the button colors in the warning message were misleading and almost mistakenly clicked no. This led us to believe that we placed too much emphasis on the "no" button and that the strong color contrast was overkill for error prevention.

Conclusion

What did I learn?

1) User research is a must: My team and I were stumped on important design decisions along the way because there were too many ambiguities that we did not address early on with user research. I learned that good design requires a strong understanding of user needs.

2) Test early on: Performing usability testing at the end revealed unexpected insights about our prototype that would have greatly improved the quality of our product had we discovered them sooner. Building in user testing as often and early as possible in my process ensures that I am receptive to feedback and my user's needs.

3) Stick to the essence: Without a clear specification of the product requirements, it can be easy to lose sight of what the core essence of the product is. Defining functional use cases provided constraints that helped us focus on the major features and design a cohesive product.
